this past winter, a professor i was working under offered to set me up on a free trip to china through a university in hebei province. china was a place i dreamt about, but experienced only through stories and diasporic facsimile. i always imagined my first trip there to be with my family, visiting our ancestoral villages in Guangzhou. it wasn't until i was at the airport that i realized the last time i had been in a plane was when i was fifteen, flying to my great-grandmother's funeral in LA.
growing up, she lived with us for half the year. our communication was limited (i was a a child; she spoke few words of english and i spoke few in cantonese). she immigrated during ww2. when she died i felt something sever, the final living thread connecting me to this other world snapped. a million questions left unanswered.

after spending the night in beijing, i awoke in a hotel and met the rest of my travel group. a collection of professors from the university, two other college students, and a handful of highschoolers with their chinese teacher. we boarded the bus and made the 4 hour journey to shijiazhuang, the capital of hebei province, where the university is located.
the scale and depth of history in china is astounding. shijiazhuang is a city of 11 million people (3 million more than new york), but you would be hard pressed to find a non-chinese person that has heard of it. the traces of rapid industrialization and a newfound consumerist culture linger; ancient military structures and buddhist temples sit minutes away from massive, glittering malls and street markets.

wuqiao, a rural county with a population of 200,000 people, is the home of chinese acrobatics. we arrived there at night, stopping to eat dinner at a small korean restaurant. during our meal, a boy arrived, accompanied by a young man dressed in a black uniform. the boy gave each of us an orange. we were told he was the son of the director of the acrobatics school in wuqiao, which we would be visiting the next morning.
the school was bustling with activity as children as young as five years old practiced circus arts around us. instead of studying for their college entrance exams (gaokao) these children studied to become acrobats and performers. the thought of children foregoing traditional education to join the circus was surreal, seeming very at-odds with the cut-throat careerism china is associated with.

beijing was overwhelming. in the span of a couple of days we visited tiananmen square, the forbidden city, the summer palace, and the great wall. i had never been in a palace before. the beauty was almost outweighed by the disgust by such extravagent displays of wealth, you begin to appreciate the peasantry's desire to revolt.
during my time there, i did not acclimate well to the winter smog. there was something exciting about going to the pharmacy near my hotel to pick up cough drops or masks, imagining how a local resident might run these errands regularly. going alone forced me to practice the chinese i was otherwise embarrassed to attempt.

i often try to imagine life from my great-grandmothers perspective. raised in rural china, an arranged marriage at just fifteen. leaving your first-born behind in hongkong to meet your husband in los angeles. what could it have been like to witness so much staggering change in one lifetime?
what does home mean to a diasporic population? where do you go when the place you are from no long exists, and the place you live doesn't want you?

this is a narrative archive of ephmera i collected during a trip to china (dec. 2024). it is also a reflection on chinese cultural identity in the diaspora. thank you for reading!
